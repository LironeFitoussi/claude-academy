# Your First Conversation with Claude

**Estimated time:** 20 minutes

Claude is designed to work as an **intelligent collaborator**, helping you move from a blank page to a clear, structured result. It brings AI capabilities such as writing, reasoning, analysis, and research, while you provide the context, expertise, and judgment that make the output useful.

The most effective way to work with Claude is not through isolated commands, but through an ongoing conversation where you provide direction, review the response, and refine the result.

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:

* Start a new conversation with Claude and navigate the main interface.
* Write effective prompts using clear and specific language.
* Upload files and images to provide additional context.
* Use follow-up messages to refine Claude's responses.
* Personalize Claude using Memory, Styles, preferences, and model selection.

# Understanding the Claude Interface

When you open **Claude.ai**, you will see a clean workspace with a text input area near the bottom of the screen.

The sidebar provides access to several important features.

## New and Previous Conversations

You can:

* Start a new chat.
* Return to previous conversations.
* Continue working with earlier context.

Each conversation keeps its own history, allowing Claude to respond based on what has already been discussed.

## Projects

**Projects** help organize related conversations around a shared objective.

A Project can include:

* Persistent background context
* Reference documents
* Custom instructions
* Multiple related chats

Projects are useful when working on an ongoing initiative, product, client, course, or research topic.

## Artifacts

**Artifacts** allow Claude to turn ideas into reusable and shareable outputs.

Examples include:

* Documents
* Interactive tools
* Applications
* Structured content
* Prototypes

Artifacts are especially useful when the output is something you want to review, revise, share, or continue developing.

# Starting a Conversation

Every interaction with Claude begins with a **prompt**.

A prompt can be as simple as:

> Suggest five code names for a new software feature.

It can also be a complex request involving research, file analysis, writing, coding, or the creation of a structured deliverable.

The best way to communicate with Claude is similar to how you would communicate with a coworker:

* Naturally
* Clearly
* Concisely
* Conversationally

You do not need to use complicated prompt syntax. Claude performs best when it understands your situation, your objective, and the result you expect.

---

# Writing Effective Prompts

A strong prompt usually contains three elements:

1. **Set the stage**
2. **Define the task**
3. **Specify the rules**

---

## 1. Set the Stage

Explain the background Claude needs to understand the request.

Useful context may include:

* Your role
* Your organization
* The project
* The audience
* The business objective
* The problem you are trying to solve

For example:

> I am the marketing lead at an independent streaming startup, and we are preparing an investor pitch deck for Series A investors.

This tells Claude who you are, what you are working on, and why the result matters.

---

## 2. Define the Task

Clearly describe what you want Claude to do.

Common actions include:

* Write
* Analyze
* Summarize
* Research
* Compare
* Build
* Review
* Debug
* Explain
* Brainstorm

Avoid vague instructions such as:

> Help me with the market.

A clearer request would be:

> Research the independent film streaming market and identify major trends, competitor positioning, and growth opportunities.

The second prompt gives Claude a specific objective and identifies the areas that should be covered.

---

## 3. Specify the Rules

Tell Claude how the answer should be presented.

You can specify:

* Tone
* Writing style
* Length
* Structure
* Format
* Audience
* Level of detail
* Required sources
* Examples to follow
* Information to include or avoid

For example:

> Use current web research with citations. Structure the result as a professional report of no more than five pages, including an executive summary, market analysis, competitive landscape, and growth opportunities.

These rules make the expected result much clearer.

---

# Complete Prompt Example

A complete prompt using all three elements could be:

> I am the marketing lead at an independent streaming startup, and we are preparing an investor pitch deck for Series A investors. Research the current independent film streaming market and identify key trends, competitor positioning, and growth opportunities. Use current web research with citations and structure the result as a professional report of no more than five pages, including an executive summary, market analysis, competitive landscape, and growth opportunities.

This prompt works well because it includes:

* **Context:** An investor pitch for a streaming startup
* **Task:** Market research and competitive analysis
* **Rules:** Current sources, citations, professional tone, length, and required sections

# AI Fluency and the 4D Framework

The prompt structure used in this lesson is related to the **4D Framework for AI Fluency**.

The framework includes four competencies:

## Delegation

Deciding which tasks should be assigned to AI and which responsibilities should remain with the user.

## Description

Clearly explaining the task, context, requirements, and desired result.

## Discernment

Evaluating the quality, accuracy, relevance, and limitations of the AI's output.

## Diligence

Using AI responsibly, ethically, safely, and with appropriate human review.

Effective AI collaboration requires more than writing a prompt. It also requires thoughtful task selection, careful evaluation, and responsible use.

# Adding Context with File Uploads

Claude can produce more relevant responses when you provide supporting materials.

You can upload documents, images, spreadsheets, code, and other files directly into a conversation.

Claude can analyze both text and visual elements, including:

* Paragraphs
* Tables
* Charts
* Diagrams
* Screenshots
* Graphics
* Source code

Supported formats include:

* PDF
* DOCX
* CSV
* TXT
* PNG
* JPEG
* Other common document and image formats

After a file is uploaded, it appears as an attachment in the chat. Claude attempts to parse its contents and use them as context for your next prompt.

---

# Practical Uses for File Uploads

## Summarizing Documents

Upload a report and ask:

> Summarize the key findings, risks, and recommendations for a senior executive.

## Analyzing Images

Upload a screenshot and ask:

> Describe the interface and identify any usability problems.

## Reviewing Data

Upload a spreadsheet and ask:

> Identify important trends, unusual values, and possible explanations.

## Understanding Code

Upload a code file and ask:

> Explain how this code works, identify possible bugs, and recommend improvements.

## Comparing Materials

Upload multiple documents and ask:

> Compare these proposals based on cost, risk, implementation effort, and expected impact.

Providing the source material directly is often faster and more reliable than describing it manually.

# Selecting the Right Claude Model

Different Claude models are optimized for different types of work.

The selected model normally appears near the message input area. You can select another model depending on the complexity of the task.

Changing the model may begin a new conversation.

---

## Claude Sonnet

**Claude Sonnet** is the recommended choice for many everyday tasks.

It balances:

* Capability
* Speed
* Cost effectiveness
* General-purpose performance

Typical use cases include:

* Writing emails
* Summarizing documents
* Brainstorming
* Everyday analysis
* Explaining concepts
* Reviewing content
* General coding support

For straightforward tasks, Sonnet with extended thinking turned off is often sufficient.

---

## Claude Opus

**Claude Opus** is intended for highly complex tasks that require deeper reasoning.

Typical use cases include:

* Multi-step financial analysis
* Advanced technical reasoning
* Complex research
* Strategic decision analysis
* Large-scale coding tasks
* Difficult problem solving

Opus may require more processing time, but it is designed for tasks where additional reasoning depth is valuable.

---

# Extended Thinking

Some Claude models support **Extended Thinking**.

This mode allows Claude to spend more effort analyzing a problem before producing the final answer.

It is useful for:

* Complex reasoning
* Mathematical problems
* Detailed planning
* Technical architecture
* Multi-step analysis
* Difficult debugging
* Strategic decisions

Extended Thinking may increase response latency, so it is not necessary for every task.

For simple requests, use a faster model configuration. For difficult tasks, enable Extended Thinking or select a more capable model.

The goal is to choose the right level of processing for the job.

---

# Research Mode

**Research** allows Claude to conduct a broader and more systematic investigation.

For a complex research question, Claude may:

* Break the question into smaller topics.
* Investigate multiple perspectives.
* Review many sources.
* Compare findings.
* Produce a comprehensive report.
* Include citations.

Research is useful for tasks such as:

* Market analysis
* Competitive intelligence
* Policy research
* Technology comparisons
* Industry reports
* Strategic investigations

Because Research is designed for depth, it is better suited to complex questions than quick factual requests.

# Iterating on Claude's Responses

Claude conversations are designed to be iterative.

You do not need to create a perfect prompt before starting. You can begin with a clear request and improve the result through follow-up messages.

The real value of Claude often comes from several connected prompts rather than one isolated interaction.

---

## Ask Follow-Up Questions

Build on Claude's response by requesting more detail or clarification.

Examples:

> Can you expand on the second recommendation?

> What evidence supports that conclusion?

> Can you explain this for a nontechnical audience?

> What risks have not yet been considered?

Follow-up questions allow you to explore the topic without repeating the entire original prompt.

---

## Request Revisions

Tell Claude exactly what should change.

Examples:

> Make this more concise.

> Rewrite this in a more conversational tone.

> Add a comparison table.

> Reduce the response to five bullet points.

> Keep the analysis but remove unnecessary background information.

Specific feedback is more useful than simply saying that the result is incorrect.

---

## Explain What Worked and What Did Not

You can guide Claude by identifying the strongest and weakest parts of the response.

For example:

> The structure is useful, but the tone is too formal. Keep the same sections and rewrite it for a startup audience.

This preserves the useful parts while clearly identifying what needs improvement.

---

## Redirect the Conversation

When Claude misunderstands the request, correct the direction directly.

For example:

> I was asking about customer retention, not customer acquisition. Focus only on retention metrics and churn reduction.

Claude can then revise its approach using the corrected context.

---

## Edit an Earlier Prompt

You can use the pencil icon to edit and resubmit one of your previous messages.

This is useful when:

* The original prompt contained an error.
* Important context was missing.
* You want to test a different wording.
* You want Claude to regenerate the conversation from that point.

Editing an earlier prompt may be more effective than adding multiple corrective messages.

---

## Restart When Necessary

Sometimes a conversation accumulates too much irrelevant context.

Starting a new chat can help when:

* The topic has changed completely.
* Claude repeatedly follows the wrong direction.
* Previous instructions conflict with the new task.
* You want a fresh result without earlier assumptions.

Use follow-up messages for normal refinement and a new chat when the conversation context itself has become unhelpful.

# Personalizing Claude

Claude includes several features that allow it to adapt to your preferences over time.

---

## Personal Preferences

You can add general instructions under:

**Settings > General > What personal preferences should Claude consider?**

Preferences may include:

* Preferred tone
* Desired response length
* Formatting expectations
* Technical experience
* Writing conventions
* Information you want Claude to avoid
* How detailed explanations should be

These preferences can apply across conversations.

---

## Memory

**Memory** stores important context from previous interactions.

Claude may remember information such as:

* Your role
* Your industry
* Your preferences
* Previous decisions
* Recurring projects
* Your typical working style

For example, after learning that you work in marketing for a B2B software company, Claude may use that context in future conversations without requiring you to repeat it.

You can review, edit, or delete stored memories in Settings.

Memory can also synchronize across devices when you are signed in.

---

## Styles

**Styles** control how Claude communicates.

You can choose preset options such as:

* Concise
* Formal
* Explanatory

You can also create a custom style.

A custom style could instruct Claude to:

* Use short paragraphs.
* Prefer direct language.
* Avoid unnecessary jargon.
* Include examples.
* Format technical content with headings and code blocks.
* Write for a specific audience.

Once selected, the style can apply automatically across conversations.

# Best Practices for Your First Conversation

## Start with a Clear Goal

Tell Claude what result you are trying to achieve.

Instead of:

> Help me write something.

Use:

> Draft a professional email asking a client to confirm the revised project deadline.

---

## Provide Relevant Context

Include information that affects the answer.

For example:

* Who the audience is
* Why the task matters
* What has already happened
* Which constraints apply
* What source material should be used

---

## Define the Output

Specify the format you want.

Examples include:

* Email
* Report
* Checklist
* Table
* Code sample
* Presentation outline
* Executive summary
* Step-by-step guide

---

## Attach Useful Materials

Upload files rather than manually describing large amounts of background information.

Relevant files improve accuracy and reduce unnecessary back-and-forth.

---

## Review the Response Carefully

Claude can produce useful work, but you should still evaluate:

* Accuracy
* Completeness
* Relevance
* Tone
* Source quality
* Assumptions
* Possible risks

Your expertise remains essential.

---

## Continue the Conversation

Do not stop after the first response.

Ask Claude to:

* Clarify
* Expand
* Shorten
* Reorganize
* Compare
* Verify
* Add examples
* Challenge assumptions
* Produce another version

Frequent communication helps you reach a result that more closely matches your needs.

# Example Conversation

## Initial Prompt

> I am a QA lead preparing a test strategy for a new e-commerce checkout flow. Create a structured test plan covering functional testing, integration testing, security risks, accessibility, performance, and regression testing. Use a professional tone and organize the output into objectives, scope, test areas, risks, and exit criteria.

## Follow-Up Prompt

> Expand the security section and include authentication, payment handling, session management, and input validation.

## Revision Prompt

> Convert the test areas into a table with columns for test category, objective, example scenarios, priority, and automation suitability.

## Final Refinement

> Add a concise executive summary for engineering managers and product stakeholders.

This sequence demonstrates how a broad request can be refined into a highly targeted deliverable.

# Common Prompting Mistakes

## Being Too Vague

Weak prompt:

> Write a report.

Improved prompt:

> Write a two-page project status report for senior management covering progress, risks, budget status, upcoming milestones, and decisions required.

---

## Omitting the Audience

A technical explanation for developers should differ from one written for executives or customers.

Always identify the target audience when it affects tone or depth.

---

## Combining Too Many Unrelated Tasks

A prompt with several unrelated goals may produce a shallow response.

Break complex work into stages:

1. Analyze the source material.
2. Identify the key findings.
3. Create the recommended structure.
4. Draft the final content.
5. Review and refine it.

---

## Expecting the First Answer to Be Final

AI collaboration is usually most effective when treated as a process.

The first response is often a starting point, not the completed result.

---

## Providing Files Without Instructions

Do not upload a file and assume Claude knows what you need.

Add a clear request such as:

> Review this document and identify contradictions, missing requirements, and unclear acceptance criteria.

# Put It into Practice

Before continuing, start a conversation with Claude using a real task from your work.

Use the following template:

> I am a **[role]** working on **[project or objective]**.
> I need you to **[specific action]**.
> Use **[source material or context]**.
> Present the result as **[format]** for **[audience]**.
> Follow these rules: **[tone, length, structure, constraints, or examples]**.

After Claude responds, continue with at least two follow-up prompts:

1. Ask for clarification, expansion, or deeper analysis.
2. Request a specific revision to the tone, format, or level of detail.
